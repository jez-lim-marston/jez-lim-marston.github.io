About

I am a physician-scientist and virologist working where molecular virology, computational genomics, and AI/ML meet, and increasingly where that intersection becomes a biosecurity question.

My research reconstructs how viruses and virus-derived elements behave in the human genome. As a postdoctoral researcher in the Cesarman Lab at Weill Cornell Medicine, I study EBV+ lymphoma and EBV/HIV co-infection at single-cell resolution. During my PhD in the Nixon Lab, I built the first locus-specific single-cell atlas of transposable element and endogenous retrovirus expression across the human immune system.

I also work hands-on under BSL-2+ conditions with HIV-1, EBV, and SARS-CoV-2, and I use AI tools in my own research every day. That combination has given me a practical understanding of how AI/ML lowers the technical barrier to dual-use work, and why thoughtful evaluation and guardrails matter. I believe AI can meaningfully advance biomedicine, and that realizing that promise safely depends on people who understand both the biology and the tools.

Alongside this core program, I follow and contribute to related work in skin immunology, antiviral immunology, autoimmunity, and computational biology more broadly — areas that intersect naturally with viral latency, endogenous retroviruses, and immune dysregulation. More on Research.
